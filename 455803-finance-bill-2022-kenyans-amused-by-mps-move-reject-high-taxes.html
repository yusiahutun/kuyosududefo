Finance Bill 2022: Kenyans Amused by MPs' Move to Reject High Taxes

Publish date: 2024-03-24
  • The National Assembly's Finance and Planning Committee, chaired by Gladys Wanga rejected the introduction of VAT on maize, wheat flour and cassava
  • The legislators equally rejected the proposed 3% digital services tax (DST), retaining it at 1.5%
  • Kenyans claimed the move by MPs was not genuine describing it as a populistic ahead of the August 9 General Election

Kenyan members of parliament have rejected several measures proposed in the Finance Bill 2022 that sought to increase taxes on excisable items, including basic food commodities.

The National Assembly's Finance and Planning Committee said the introduction of Value Added Tax (VAT) on items such as maize, wheat flour and cassava would overburden Kenyans more.

The National Treasury wanted the items deleted from the tax-exempt list and subjected to a 16% VAT.

“The committee observed that the amendment will increase the price of maize flour, cassava and wheat flour,” Wanga said, as reported by Citizen Digital.

Kenyans on social media were amused after the MPs rejected the taxes, arguing it could be one way to seek re-election in the forthcoming August 9 General Election.

TUKO.co.ke sampled some reactions:

Baraza Wangila noted:

"They have been in office for quite a long time. The prices of basic commodity prices are already up."

Martin Muiruri lamented:

"We know you, politicians; you have waited for 10 years for Kenyans to languish with the high prices. What you want to do, know you could have done it long ago. Don't blindfold us, please."

List of taxes rejected by MPs

1. They rejected 16% VAT on maize, cassava and wheat flour.

2. The MPs shot down a 10% excise duty on beer.

3. They also rejected 20% excise duty on wines and spirits.

4. A 15% excise duty on liquor advertising was also rejected.

5. A 25% excise duty on glass bottles suffered the same fate.

6. Yatani's proposed 15% excise duty on advertisements on betting also flopped.

7. The legislators equally rejected the proposed 3% digital services tax (DST), retaining it at 1.5%.
