Interview with E.L.V Denim founder

Publish date: 2024-02-12

When it comes to sustainable fashion, it can be hard to know where to start. To help, every month we focus on a brand that knows exactly what it means to be a responsible force for good in fashion today.

This month we’re getting to know E.L.V Denim, a British label crafting timeless yet statement designs from 100 per cent upcycled materials, therefore giving life to garments and preventing them from ending up in landfill.

"Even waste can become items of beauty," says sustainable designer Anna Foster whose label E.L.V Denim sells upcycled jeans, shirts, dresses and accessories. "The brand was born from an ethos of conscious design, local manufacturing, and slow production," she tells us.

E.L.V Denim – which stands for East London Vintage Denim – launched in 2018, first with jeans, before expanding into a wider range of products. It uses 100 per cent recycled fabrics and only uses non-stretch denim so that the clothing will last a lifetime.

"I'm proud to be a thought leader in this space, however, this journey is not just for me and E.L.V. Denim. We want to lead by example and show others that upcycling can be incorporated with great design and luxury tailoring – this can be a successful business model. We all need to be part of this change."

She's right. As consumers, it is the responsibility of each one of us to do our bit and make positive changes to look after our planet. With a staggering estimated 1.75 million tonnes of clothing and textile waste created in the UK each year and 1.2 million tonnes of it ending up in landfill, it's time we take charge. If every person made even just one small change to how they consume fashion; opting for secondhand, renting one-off pieces for special occasions or recycling their clothes after they're done with them, it would make a huge difference. "It’s better for everyone to be one per cent better than one person to be 100 per cent better," says Foster.

Below, we chat to the designer about her concerns over the denim industry, how things have changed since starting her label and her advice for building a greener wardrobe.

Why is the denim industry, in particular, harmful to the planet?

"For decades the denim industry has been exhausting the environment with its intense water, dyeing and texturing processes [when jeans are manipulated to imitate natural rips and fade]. Coupled with the extreme mass scale of denim production, I felt there was a real need to address the over-abundant supply not being utilised. As a world population, we produce four billion pairs of jeans a year, the majority of which are discarded.

"Just because the size, shape or condition makes the jeans undesirable it doesn’t mean that the denim material itself doesn’t have value and shouldn’t mean the material is rendered unusable. Rigid denim was originally designed as a working fabric and is made to last. Therefore, it’s the perfect material to rework, giving it a new life in sophisticated and timeless silhouettes and allowing it to attain its perfect potential."

What should customers look out for when shopping for sustainable denim?

"Always choose rigid denim, which is denim fabric woven from 100 per cent cotton fibres without any additions of elastane. Stretch denim will always eventually lose shape, so people end up discarding them and buying another pair which creates a viscous cycle. All E.L.V. Denim jeans are made from upcycled rigid denim, and we also incorporate couture tailoring into our designs to ensure jeans are the perfect fit. Our recognisable design is not only an aesthetic one, but also a functional one, as the four seams have an allowance which means you can easily take in or out your jeans according to your life."

What do you think needs to change in the fashion industry?

"We need to encourage others to champion local manufacturing and slow fashion. I'm proud to be a thought leader in this space, however, this journey is not just for me and E.L.V. Denim. We want to lead by example and show others that upcycling can be incorporated with great design and luxury tailoring – this can be a successful business model. We all need to be part of this change."

What do you want to achieve personally with your brand, in terms of sustainability?

"E.L.V. Denim was born from an ethos of conscious design, local manufacturing, and slow production. We don’t use any new, virgin material in any of our designs ensuring that we have zero impact on the environment. However, we are constantly raising our standards and always trying to be better. We have invested in innovation so we are able to upcycle textiles from other industries besides denim, and we proudly launched our 'White Collection' last summer, taking damaged linen from the luxury hotel industry. Just like denim, this sumptuous linen can have a new life and a story.

"We are now collecting all our threads and tiny little scraps and turning them into our swing tags and we save all the old buttons and rivets and are working to turn them into 'Objet d’art' – even our waste can become items of beauty."

Arash Davoodi

Do you think our attitudes to fashion have changed since you started your brand? If so, how?

"I have certainly seen a change in attitudes in the last five years. When I launched the brand in 2018 there were relatively few people championing an environmental shift in the industry, but now we are actively seeing a change in mindset. Customers are now mindful of how harmful the fashion industry can be and that there are alternatives, they are looking for brands that embody an ethical ethos from its core. People are also seeking transparency and individuality, and by upcycling vintage denim each E.L.V. Denim jeans has its own unique characteristics which are inherently one of a kind. Coupled with our couture tailoring, we create handcrafted luxury items that last a lifetime and that is certainly something to be celebrated."

What advice would you give to those wanting to make their business sustainable?

"Ask questions all the time; the main being can we do better? A sustainable business is one that puts community, design and quality over pure profit. Those are high standards to maintain but if you know your business is creating products of high quality which are desirable and designed to last, then these are the foundations for longevity and success.

"I base my business decisions on moral values and common-sense innovation. We all can reason that purchasing a locally made upcycled handcrafted brand is better socially, economically, and environmentally. If you produce locally your carbon footprint is lower and you are supporting the local community. And if you are upcycling you are revaluing garments and materials that deserve to be given a second life, and not taking valuable resources from the environment."

What’s the smallest change a consumer could make to become more eco-conscious?

"Don’t buy stretch denim, as unfortunately it isn’t designed to last and when it is discarded it doesn’t decompose. The elastane used has a huge long-lasting impact on our planet – this is a very easy step which everybody can take to becoming more eco-conscious.

"Don’t throw textile waste in the bin, it takes a little longer to go the distance to put it in a fabric bin (you can contact your local council to find your nearest one). That isn’t the perfect solution, but this fabric has a better chance of being recycled than sent to landfill where our waste ends up. We can’t be hard on ourselves and making a tiny change has a huge impact, but it’s better for everyone to be one per cent better than one person to be 100 per cent better."

Arash Davoodi

What are the most common inaccurate “facts” about sustainability you see pushed out?

"Consumers must be wary of the narrative around sustainability that is often appropriated by those who are not always acting with integrity. We are all aware of greenwashing and it’s not always easy to decipher, but a quick look at the content on the brand's website is a fairly good indication of how dedicated they are. I would encourage people who are trying to act more eco-conscious to be more wary of fabric said to be made from recycled bottles – there aren’t enough bottles of the correct plastic to make the amount of material that is advertised.

"Additionally, denim which claims to be ‘recycled’. Recycled denim isn’t made from just recycled denim. The actual denim is a tiny proportion, as it’s made from wood pulp, hemp and other cotton. The best thing is to read the label which reveals the truth. Lastly, ‘sustainable high street fashion’ is an oxymoron."

Arash Davoodi

What’s the biggest misconception about sustainable clothing and what would you say to counteract this?

"That consciously made fashion is too expensive. For E.L.V. Denim, our jeans are handcrafted and designed to last. Buying one pair of jeans that lasts forever is better than buying multiple cheaper stretch jeans that you have to buy every year. The cost per wear of our jeans far outweighs the cost of frequent re-purchasing."

Where do you turn to when you feel confused about sustainability and need more detail?

"The Good On You online directory, Aja Barber and Venetia De La Mana."
