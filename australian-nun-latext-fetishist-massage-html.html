Australian nun becomes a latext fetishist after massage

Publish date: 2024-07-29

A former Tibetan Buddhist nun has swapped her maroon robes for something a little tighter.

Melbourne-born Damcho Dyson, 45, could once be found in monasteries in the likes of Nepal, the Himalayas, France, and South East Asia. 

Now she frequents latex clubs in South London on the weekends. 

This is the incredible story of Michelle Tonkin, who served as a nun for 10 years until she had a very different kind of spiritual awakening.

Before she chose the celibate life, Damcho was immersed in the world of art in her native Australia. 

She graduated with First Class Honours from the Victorian College of Arts and displayed her installation art throughout the country.

But when she was 23 years old, Damcho had an abortion. It was a decision that shook the artist to her very core. 

At the same time as she was struggling with the decision, Damcho began to study Tibetan Buddhism and first considered the possibility of becoming a nun.

Six years later, in Nepal, Damcho pledged to a life of celibacy and became ordained. She was 29 years old. 

For the next 10 years Damcho volunteered across the globe, even acting as an attendant for the Dalai Lama when he once visited a French monastery that served as her base.

Every day she draped herself in the maroon cloth of her monastic robes, her sole focus being 'cultivating compassion' and training her mind 'through meditation'. 

But everything changed one day while Damcho was visiting an Indian retreat in 2011. 

It was during a massage that the former nun realised just how disconnected she had become from her own body, swathed everyday in those layers of cloth.

'I’d been celibate for 10 years and, as a nun, my practice meant my brain was able to override my bodily needs,' the 45-year-old told the Sunday People.

'Lying there having that massage I had an epiphany. I suddenly had a sense of the vitality within my body and decided the time was right to leave the monastery,' she added.

Damcho found herself questioning her own motives for becoming a nun, and realised she was still punishing herself for the abortion she had nearly 20 years ago. 

'As much as I thought it came about through inspiration, renunciation, and devotion, the seed was that I was paying penance for something that I did with my body when I was 23,' she told The Huffington Post.

'I knew I had to forgive myself of that and begin to allow my body to feel, live, experience and explore again.'  

After a few months of reflection, Damcho decided she needed to give up the life of a nun for good.

Five years later, Damcho was strolling through a London market when she decided to try on a studded black latex corset. 

It was love at first touch. 

'I immediately felt hooked,' she told the Post. 'It was armour-like and cocoon-like whilst holding me securely like a determined hug.' 

Damcho befriended the stall owner, a couture designer who had made a career of creating latex clothing.

The designer encouraged Damcho to try on a few more pieces and introduced her to the latex scene. 

Despite the very different material, Damcho was struck by a sense of familiarity as she donned the rubber clothing. 

She immediately 'recognised' that, much like when she put on her robes, slipping into latex was a similar 'ritual'. 

'Whether monastic robes or putting on latex, what has become apparent to me is that bond demand commitment of the wearer,' Damcho wrote on a GoFundMe she has set up to raise funds to pursue a PhD at the Royal College of Art in London.

'Being touched while in latex, sense awareness is heightened. And once the latex is removed, there is a deeply felt release - a sense of being unbound,' she said

'Once the latex is on, the body has a second skin, it is like a cocoon. It is so close to your skin that you feel naked, yet it also is like a protective armour.' 

'Being touched while in latex, sense awareness is heightened. And once the latex is removed, there is a deeply felt release - a sense of being unbound.' 

Damcho hopes to use her time at the college to further explore the dichotomy between her two lives and explore the connection between 'femininity and ritual, boundaries and breaking them, and the different masks we wear', she explained on her GoFundMe page.

'Now I have the opportunity to translate these life experiences into a unique contribution,' she said. 

'That I believe will intrigue and - I hope - bring some benefit.'
