Avid Pro Tools vs. Logic Pro X: Which DAW Is Better?

Publish date: 2024-09-13

The age-old question of which DAW to use is a battle that continues to this day. At the top of that list are Pro Tools and Logic, two industry heavyweights that can do almost anything when it comes to editing and producing audio. So which one should you choose?

Whether you are looking for your very first DAW or considering a switch, our side-by-side comparison of Avid Pro Tools and Logic Pro X will help you determine which workstation to call home.

Head-to-Head: Pro Tools vs Logic Pro X

Both Pro Tools and Logic Pro X can do almost any kind of audio editing and production that you can imagine. There is little to worry about in terms of functionality as each software is equipped with incredible audio tools that have been developed over decades.

Logic Pro X has been around since the early 1990s while Pro Tools has been around since 1989. One major deciding factor is what operating system you currently have, and whether you are willing to switch. Logic is only available on Apple computers, while Pro Tools is available on both Windows and macOS.

However, there's more to consider than just the operating system, including price, features, and which DAW will ultimately suit you the best. Let's dive right in...

Price

Pro Tools works on a subscription basis with the choice of three price options. This means that in the short term, the cost of Pro Tools is lower than Logic and you have the added benefit of choosing a tier that suits your needs. The trial version lasts for 30 days and subscription prices start at $9.99 per month for the artist tier.

By comparison, you can buy Logic for $199 for the entire software or test it out first with a generous 90-day trial period. The downside is you will need to invest in an Apple computer or MacBook if you don't already own one. One way to lower the costs is by buying a refurbished Mac.

It's also worth noting that Logic software updates continue to be free once you've purchased Logic, which will bring plenty of new features over time at no extra cost.

System Requirements

Most DAWs require a computer with decent processing power to support the audio engine. Working with audio files or sound libraries also results in large file sizes too, so make sure you have enough storage capacity on your computer.

Pro Tools

  • Windows 10 or 11, or macOS 10.14.6 or later
  • Intel Core i5 processor or M1 on Apple computers
  • 16GB RAM (32GB or more recommended)
  • 15GB storage space for installation
  • Compatible NVIDIA graphics card (Windows)

Logic Pro X

  • macOS 11.5 or later
  • 6GB storage space for installation
  • 72GB storage space for complete sound library installation
  • iOS 14.0 or later for iPad and iPhone to use Logic Remote

Standout Features

One standout feature of Pro Tools is Beat Detective, a function that automatically detects beats in an audio recording. Use it to apply batch cuts and fades to an entire track to save you a ton of editing time. You can also use it to quantize audio just like you can with MIDI.

Combine it with a tool like Elastic Audio and you can effortlessly fix, stretch, or warp any recorded audio. Besides making your recordings sound "tight", you can also do creative things like quantizing a live-drum beat to make it sound like a drum machine or sample.

When it comes to Logic, MIDI music is where it stands out the most. Use Live Loops to build songs on-the-fly by playing samples in a modular, drag-and-drop style grid. Or mess around with the powerful Alchemy synthesizer to craft unique never-before-heard sounds.

The virtual Drummer can't be overlooked either. Use it to drop in fully formed drum tracks that match the genre and groove you want. It's perfect for mocking up demo tracks when you don't have a real drummer to record.

These DAWs share a lot of functions but they clearly have different goals. Pro Tools really excels in all things audio-related, while Logic Pro X has made sure its MIDI functions are the best.

Ease of Use

Since both DAWs have extensive functionality, choosing between Pro Tools or Logic often comes down to how easy it is to use in your workflow. Some people enjoy the slick look of Apple apps and will find Logic's interface to be pleasing to navigate. While on the flip side, the straightforward workspace in Pro Tools gives you complete control over audio editing.

If you're upgrading from GarageBand to Logic, the transition couldn't be more smooth. Both DAWs look almost identical, so navigating Logic will be quite familiar. Adding to this, you can turn advanced features off while you get up to speed with the fundamentals first.

Because Logic focuses on MIDI music-making, you can expect the MIDI editing window to be intuitive and easy to use. Because not everyone has an external MIDI controller either, there is an option to use your computer keyboard to play software instruments too.

By comparison, Pro Tools can look rather complicated on the surface, but this won't be a huge dealbreaker if you are already familiar with using a fully-featured DAW. Tools aren't hidden in menus as they are in Logic, but rather laid out in the editing timeline for quick access.

Simple functions like zooming in on audio waveforms to make a cut, or dragging a corner of the region to add a fade, can feel a lot more simple in Pro Tools. This makes a huge difference when you have numerous vocal tracks to comp, or drum tracks to edit.

Who Does It Suit the Most?

While both DAWs are equally used by professionals, it is clear that some people may benefit more from choosing one over the other.

Logic Pro X: For Singer-Songwriters, Beat Makers, and Bedroom Producers

At its core, Logic can be seen as an empowering creative tool for making new tracks. While it can be more annoying to make fine edits to audio using this DAW, the MIDI functions are fantastic for those who work with MIDI music.

With a host of really strong plugins, software synthesizers, virtual instruments, and a huge loop library, the best part of buying Logic is that you won't need to make third-party purchases for some time.

Whether you are migrating from GarageBand or using its built-in tutorials, learning how to use Logic is made to be easy. It's clear that for beginners, Logic Pro is a user-friendly DAW with all the tools necessary to create music from the start.

If you can't afford access to a studio, or third-party plugins/instruments are not yet in your price range, then Logic offers a serious bang for your buck. For musicians and bedroom producers, Logic is both a powerful creative tool and a fully-featured professional DAW that will help you go from amateur to pro.

Pro Tools: For Engineers, Producers, and Audiophiles

While Pro Tools can't compete on the MIDI front, it does offer the best workstation for all things audio-related. Its powerful audio engine combined with its efficient editing workspace makes Pro Tools the go-to option for recording, editing, mixing, and mastering audio tracks.

For aspiring audio engineers or producers, you may also want to learn Pro Tools to give you the option of collaborating with other people in the music industry who no doubt use Pro Tools. As for audiophiles, Pro Tools offers the most attractive option for editing audio with micro-level precision.

Its focus on multi-track recording means that Pro Tools is one of the best options for recording everything from rock bands to jazz music to choirs and orchestras. It's a confident choice for when you have an extensive recording session to edit, which is why this DAW is used in many professional studios in the music industry.

Compared to Logic, Pro Tools is more like a blank canvas, perfect for anyone who wants complete control over how they handle audio. Choose the DAW That Suits Your Needs

At the end of the day, both DAWs are equally jam-packed with advanced features, so it will take you the same amount of hours to get used to working in either software. It might be as simple as choosing the DAW that works on your operating system or picking the DAW with the standout feature you want.

Everyone is different, but luckily, whatever option you choose, both Pro Tools and Logic Pro X have almost everything you need in a top-tier DAW.
